Abstract
The anomalous behavior of water (H₂O) has long been attributed to differences in electronegativity among its constituent atoms. In this study, we propose an alternative framework in which molecular polarity primarily arises from the interactions of electrical fields generated by the atoms themselves. We argue that while electronegativity differences contribute to the final polarity of a molecule, they do not directly induce shifts in electron density; rather, it is the mutual influence of atomic electrical fields that governs this redistribution. Central to our model is the concept of hydrogen bonding acting as a "proximity switch." When water molecules form hydrogen bonds, the opposing electrical fields partially cancel one another, effectively neutralizing a portion of the polar force. Conversely, upon dissociation of these bonds, the polar forces are reactivated. This dynamic modulation of polarity offers a novel explanation for over 70 persistent anomalies observed in water’s behavior—phenomena that have defied conventional interpretations.
Discussion
The complex balance of repulsive and attractive forces among polar molecules—exemplified by water (H₂O)—underlies their orderly arrangement through hydrogen bonding. In this study, we begin by posing a key question: Are the intermolecular forces between water molecules mainly due to differences in the electronegativity of their constituent atoms, as professed by Linus Pauling in the 1950s, or do they originate from the electrical fields those atoms produce? The prevailing view favors the former, but this assumption has led to over 70 unresolved (and seemingly unresolvable) anomalies that continue to mount as new research uncovers behaviors of H₂O that lie outside the predictions of the current model. We contend instead that molecular polarity arises from the electrical fields generated by the atoms.
Although polar molecules do exhibit electronegativity differences—in water, for example, the hydrogen atoms are less electronegative than the oxygen atom—these differences are only part of the story. They help shape the final polarity but do not directly trigger the shifts in electron density. Rather, it is the interaction of the atoms’ electrical fields upon each other that drives these changes. The field produced by the covalently bonded hydrogen atoms causes a redistribution of electron density on the oxygen atom, and the oxygen’s field similarly influences the electron density around each hydrogen atom. The net result of these asymmetric interactions is a polar molecule. Thus, it is the electrical fields—not just the differences in electronegativity—that fundamentally determine molecular polarity.
This misattribution in the current paradigm has led to a critical conceptual oversight, which we address in this paper. Specifically, we show that hydrogen bond formation effectively serves as a “proximity switch.” When a hydrogen bond is established, a portion of the polar force is neutralized by the direct opposition of the interacting electrical fields. When the bond breaks, however, the polar forces are reactivated. This switching mechanism, wherein hydrogen bonding modulates the effective polarity of the molecules, offers a fresh explanation for the phenomena traditionally termed the “anomalies of H₂O.” Central to our model is the well-known principle that opposing electrical gradients cancel each other. When two water molecules form a hydrogen bond, segments of their electrical fields oppose one another, leading to a partial cancellation of polarity in both molecules.
We recognize that this reinterpretation of hydrogen bonding—as a dynamic modulation of polarity—might be confusing for several reasons:
1. The challenge of visualizing the electrical gradients around atoms;
2. The complex tetrahedral geometry of the water molecule, which complicates understanding its hydrogen bonding; and
3. A general oversight regarding how opposing electrical gradients cancel each other.
To clarify these points, the following figures and exhibits will:
* Clearly depict the effect of electrical gradients around atoms, showing the distribution of positive and negative charges;
* Introduce a simplified model of a polar molecule—composed of a hydrogen atom and a hypothetical second species with only two valence electrons (a heuristic device not found in nature)—to sidestep the complications introduced by Oxygen’s tetrahedral arrangement of loci on it’s outer valence; and
* Emphasize the collapse of opposing electrical gradients when molecules come into close proximity during hydrogen bonding of compound molecules.

As indicated above, the purpose of this heuristic atom is to avoid the explanatory complexities of this tetrahedral arrangement of loci.

Conclusion
This breakthrough offers a long-awaited respite from the oversimplified models that stemmed from Pauling’s misguided assumption that polarity arises solely from electronegativity differences between the oxygen and hydrogen atoms in the H₂O molecule. With this new perspective, we are no longer constrained by the notion that water’s polarity and hydrogen bonding can be explained away as a weak and idiosyncratic ionic bond. Nor must we treat the remarkable anomalies of H₂O as outliers or dismiss them with inadequate explanations of water’s “surface tension,” which have historically failed to capture the true complexity of water’s glasslike surface.
Furthermore, we need not rely on the simplistic idea that, at some particular temperature, water molecules spontaneously “stand” atop one another to form ice. Instead, we can now recognize that structural hardness in water arises from a relatively balanced distribution of broken and formed hydrogen bonds. This insight opens new avenues for understanding phenomena that were once thought inexplicable. For example, the formation of spinning, rigid polymer-like chains of H₂O along wind shear boundaries can now be seen as giving rise to stable plasma-like structures that envelop flow phenomena, leading to the formation of vortices.
These vortices, in turn, serve as conduits for the contained flow of jet streams—rich in energetic, low-pressure air—delivering these conditions to storm systems via well-defined channels. Such an integrated understanding of H₂O’s structural and dynamical properties paves the way for a deeper comprehension of water’s role in atmospheric and fluid phenomena, moving beyond the limitations imposed by oversimplified theories and toward a more nuanced appreciation of water’s capabilities.
Summary

While traditional interpretations have emphasized differences in electronegativity as the primary driver of molecular polarity, our analysis reveals that the electrical fields generated by atoms—and their dynamic modulation through hydrogen bonding—play a pivotal role. This new perspective not only redefines our understanding of water’s anomalous properties but also provides a coherent framework for resolving long-standing conceptual challenges in the study of polar molecular interactions.
