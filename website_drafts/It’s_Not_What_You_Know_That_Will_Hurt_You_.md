It’s Not What You Know That Will Hurt You . . .  
. . . it’s What You Think You Know That Just Ain't So.
By James McGinn
Groupthink plays a significant role in how the human brain processes information. Often, when someone shares information with you, and that information contains an underlying assumption, you tend to adopt the same assumption without realizing it. This occurs at a subconscious level, so you’re barely aware of it. Take the following response to a Quora question as an example—see if you can spot the hidden assumption:
Question: What causes tornadoes?
Answer by Gabriel Garfield: "There are many theories for what causes tornadoes, but they are untested. The VORTEX 2 field project, which I am a part of, aims to surround tornadoes with various weather instruments to try and understand why they form. We know that the parent thunderstorm causing a tornado—i.e., a supercell—forms due to a combination of buoyancy and wind shear. Something similar likely happens with a tornado, but the exact mechanisms are still unknown."
Did you catch the hidden assumption? Consider this part: "We know that the parent thunderstorm causing a tornado—i.e., a supercell—forms due to a combination of buoyancy and wind shear." Do we really know thunderstorms are the parent of tornadoes? Do we know buoyancy is a factor? (For that matter, do we know moist air is more buoyant than dry air?) The answer to all of these is no, we don’t actually know—these ideas are as untested as the theories Gabriel mentions.
This issue isn’t new in science. Ideally, the scientific method involves professionals continually challenging and testing assumptions. But in reality, certain assumptions persist unchallenged through generations, eventually becoming sacred and unquestionable. At that point, questioning these ideas invites pushback from experts who have grown comfortable with them, to the point that they might even silence dissent. These experts morph into gatekeepers of “truth,” working hard to obscure their beliefs behind increasingly complex, scientific-sounding jargon.
For example, consider this paper by professional meteorologists advising against journalists using the phrase “clash of air masses” when explaining tornado origins:
Tornadoes in the Central United States and the 'Clash of Air Masses' by David M. Schultz, Yvette P. Richardson, Paul M. Markowski, and Charles A. Doswell III.
In it, the authors outline what is supposedly known about storms and tornado formation:
"...storms occur when warm humid air near the surface is capped by drier air at higher altitudes, with temperature decreasing rapidly with height [originating from higher terrain to the west or southwest]. This creates instability, providing energy for storms. Large changes in wind with height ('wind shear') over both shallow (lowest 1 km) and deep (lowest 6 km) layers—combined with the instability and high humidity near the surface—create conditions favorable for tornado formation."
And:
"...convective storms initiate when air parcels with convective available potential energy (CAPE) reach their level of free convection (LFC), often triggered by ascent linked to airmass boundaries..."
Does any of this actually explain anything? Aren’t these statements just observations wrapped in complex terminology? It seems like a lot of circular reasoning: updrafts happen because air moves upward; instability results from air being unstable; winds increase with height. Ultimately, one might argue that these statements only serve to obscure the reality that meteorology doesn't fully understand storms or tornadoes.
