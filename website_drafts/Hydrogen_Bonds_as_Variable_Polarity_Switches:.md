	2. The Missing Principle
Each hydrogen bond neutralises exactly ¼ of a water molecule’s dipole.
 Water can form up to four bonds; its residual polarity therefore ranges from 100 % (no bonds) to 0 % (all bonds satisfied).

	3. How Bonds Cancel Dipoles
1. Simple symmetry (H-centric) — When a hydrogen atom donates a bond, the acceptor’s opposing field relaxes the stretched H-electron cloud.

2. Tetrahedral symmetry (O-centric) — When the oxygen accepts a bond, its charge distribution becomes perfectly tetrahedral, extinguishing the O-dipole.

Every bond therefore creates one simple + one tetrahedral symmetry event, turning water into a self-neutralising array whose net polarity depends on how many bonds remain incomplete.
4. Anomalies Resolved
Anomaly
	Variable-Polarity Explanation
	Low viscosity
	In bulk liquid most molecules carry ≤ 50 % polarity; forces drop to zero at full contact, so they glide past one another.
	High freezing point
	Ice forms when bond breakage re-activates enough polarity to generate a rigid 3-D “surface tension” within the crystal.
	Very high boiling point
	Boiling requires stripping all four bonds, overcoming the full 100 % dipole force.
	Exceptional heat capacity
	Continuous bond-making/breaking converts kinetic energy into reversible electrostatic work—molecules bounce instead of transmitting heat.
	Record surface tension
	Surface molecules are capped at three bonds → 25 % polarity → strong, quasi-covalent 2-D network.
	5. Atmospheric Pay-off
   * Moist air is mostly non-polar because droplets are fully bonded—hence their surprising passivity.

   * Wind shear elongates droplets, breaking bonds and re-activating polarity along nanothreads. These plasma-like filaments provide the structural “muscle” behind tornadoes and hurricane eyewalls.

6. Take-Home Law
Polarity = 100 % × (1 – 0.25 × # bonds).
No bonds → maximum polarity; four bonds → zero polarity.
This single rule unifies water’s 70 + anomalies and invites a wholesale re-thinking of meteorology, climatology, and materials science.
