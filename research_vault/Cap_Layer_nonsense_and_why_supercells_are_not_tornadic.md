The idea of a "cap" layer blocking upwelling air ( https://youtu.be/IxOaNNndZVw?si=zBvjnEOfm8ut45Qr&t=126 ) is based on a rather questionable interpretation of smoke rising and then appearing to be blocked from further ascent. In reality, the smoke isn’t blocked; instead, the drier air in the so-called "inversion" layer lacks sufficient static charge to attract the smoke particles, causing the smoke to disperse rather than continue rising. Thus, the concept of cap layers doesn’t hold up. Dry air, lacking structural properties, cannot physically block upwelling air. On the other hand, calm moist air does exhibit slight structural properties due to the surface tension of its suspended nanodroplets in combination with static charge, but this effect is very minimal. Under conditions involving wind shear, this effect can become amplified, which contributes to the formation of vortex plasmas. However, it’s important not to overemphasize the role of static electricity; the amplification is more directly linked to increased surface tension of H₂O nanodroplets under high-energy wind shear.
Moist air is consistently denser than drier air in its vicinity, meaning that claims of moist air being inherently buoyant are nonsense. However, moist air can still rise under certain circumstances. In the lower troposphere, calm moist air is sometimes able to ascend due to the influence of static electricity, but this ascent generally tops out around the altitude where "inversion" layers form. (The term "inversion" itself is somewhat misleading, as the drier layers above can sometimes be warmer, but this is not always the case, meaning it does not always constitute a true inversion of adiabatic cooling.)
Moist air can also be lifted higher into the troposphere through vortex activity associated with storms. This is especially true when a vortex does not find a direct pathway to the ground to form a tornado. Instead, the vortex remains aloft, generating significant low pressure that can lift moist air in bulk, leading to the formation of dramatic spinning supercells and wall clouds. This phenomenon helps explain why large, visually impressive supercells often do not produce significant tornadoes.
