What Pauling got right about hydrogen bonding in water
Linus Pauling understood many aspects of hydrogen bonding between water molecules.
* He understood the Oxygen molecule as possessing two non bonding, paired electrons on its outer valence and two unpaired electrons that became paired when they formed covalent bonds with two Hydrogen atoms.
* He understood that since the outer valence of each hydrogen atom was so close to its nucleus that this set the stage for electromagnetic lopsidedness when hydrogen atoms were covalently attached to larger atoms. This resulted in the H2O molecule being “polar”.
* He understood that the arrangement of electron pairs around the nucleus of the oxygen atom is tetrahedral, which set the stage for a three dimensional component to how H2O molecules attached to each. (Note: this brought complications with respect to representing H2O on flat paper. These complications are a source of confusion for students up to the present.)
* He understood that this same tetrahedral arrangement of electrons on the oxygen atom allowed each H2O molecule the ability to potentially form four hydrogen bonds, one each with up to four neighboring H2O molecules.
* He understood two of these four locations, where the hydrogen is attached, being predominantly positive in charge and the other two, on the body of the oxygen molecule, being predominantly negative in charge.
* He understood that because of this distinction in charges that this would dictate a general consistency or symmetry in the way H2O molecules attached to each other, in accordance with the standard rules of negative and positive attraction and repulsion
(Demonstrate an Oxygen molecule. Show two attached hydrogen. Visually demonstrate the electrical gradients, two positive and two negative, protruding from their respective locations on the H2O molecule.)
* He correctly understood the bonding relationship being hydrogen bonds as involving touching between water molecules, similar to an ionic bond, and not bonded pairs as in covalent bonding. And he understood these charges as being partial and thus there existed an upper limit to the strength of hydrogen bonds
* Also, Pauling employed a somewhat arbitrary but not imprecise notion of electronegativity to describe the source of the charge differences that underlie the strength of the bonds that form between H2O molecules. This was especially useful as heuristic for introducing the concept of hydrogen bonding to students.
