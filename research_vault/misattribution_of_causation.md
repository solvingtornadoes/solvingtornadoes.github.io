When Science Misattributes Cause: How a Common Conceptual Error Kept Water Science and Meteorology in the Dark for Decades
James McGinn / Solving Tornadoes
Can a misunderstanding about causation cause an entire scientific field to spin its wheels, going nowhere for decades? Science, for all its rigor, is vulnerable to conceptual errors, and one of these errors has profoundly hindered our understanding of water and, consequently, the very nature of weather itself.
Consider a seemingly straightforward question: Can a difference, in and of itself, ever be causal? Reflect carefully on this. It might seem intuitive—differences are constantly cited as reasons for occurrences. But the correct answer is subtle yet crucial: a difference alone, whether qualitative or quantitative, cannot be inherently causal. Common parlance frequently treats differences as causal, but this shorthand typically presumes an underlying causal mechanism already understood. Unfortunately, this linguistic shortcut has fostered a pervasive scientific delusion—that differences alone can generate causation.
This conceptual error traces back to Linus Pauling's explanation for the polarity of water molecules in the 1950s. Pauling famously stated that water's polarity is caused by the electronegativity difference between its oxygen and hydrogen atoms. While it's true that oxygen and hydrogen differ in electronegativity—and indeed, this difference is instrumental—this difference itself does not directly cause the shift of electrons relative to the nuclei that defines polarity. Instead, it's the electrical gradients emanating from the oxygen atom that shifts the electron location on the hydrogen atoms, and conversely, it's the electrical gradients emanating from Hydrogen atoms that shifts the electron location on the oxygen atom. Differences in electronegativity cause the shift in the oxygen to be more negative and the shift in the hydrogen to be more positive. Thus, the difference in electronegativity is instrumental but not fundamentally causal. The true causal agents are these electrical gradients.
Why does this subtle distinction matter so deeply? Because when water molecules form hydrogen bonds, their electrical gradients directly oppose each other. In keeping with the standard rules of chemistry, when electrical gradients oppose one another they cancel out each other’s effect. Specifically, with each and every hydrogen bond electrical gradients from both molecules are brought into opposition, thereby neutralizing an incremental percentage of each other’s polarity, 25% per bond with each water molecule capable of forming up to four hydrogen bonds. Through the polarity neutralizing effects of these “hydrogen bonds,” water acts as a solvent to its own polarity, effectively turning polarity off and on incrementally with the formation and dissipation (breakage) of hydrogen bonds. The failure to recognize this has led scientists to label the ensuing unexpected behaviors that result from this mixture of hydrogen bonding causing polarity neutralization and bond dissipation causing activation as "anomalous," perpetuating confusion and misunderstanding.
Water, therefore, is not anomalous—it's simply misunderstood. The origin of this misunderstanding lies in incorrectly attributing causation to a mere difference. Far from being trivial, this error has had enormous implications, particularly for meteorology. Understanding weather patterns and atmospheric vortices—what might be called the "plumbing" of our atmosphere—depends fundamentally on understanding water’s surface tension.
At the top of the troposphere, moist and dry air meet under conditions known as wind shear. Here, vortices form, as they grow, often trending downward along more vertical wind shear boundaries, funneling energetic low pressure downward to produce uplift, cloud formation and storms and siphoning air from lower altitudes upward, exhausting their flow into jet streams (thereby explaining how vortices function to maintain the momentum of the flow of jet streams). To grasp how these vortices arise and function, one must first appreciate surface tension. And, along these lines, consider that this phenomenon, “surface tension” was originally labelled as such because water molecules along the surface of liquid water are commonly observed to be attached to each other, producing a thin, weak but structurally stable surface.  We can now say why: the surface of liquid water experiences incomplete hydrogen bonding—thus reactivating polarity and generating the cohesive forces observed in the H2O molecules on the surface of liquid water.
Hopefully you understand that what has been stated up to this point in this article is that to fully comprehend surface tension one must first correctly understand water’s polarity. If the cause of polarity is misattributed the entire conceptual framework collapses. Without recognizing that it is electrical gradients—rather than merely differences in electronegativity—that create polarity, and without understanding how hydrogen bonds incrementally neutralize this polarity, one cannot grasp the full extent of water’s surface tension capabilities—capabilities which, in the context of the unique conditions of atmospheric wind shear, are capable of much higher magnitude than that associated with the relatively subtle form of surface tension that occurs on the surface of liquid water. It is this higher magnitude form of surface tension (the  details of which are outside the scope of this article) which serves as the basis of the structural properties of atmospheric vortices, and without which one cannot hope to decipher the fundamental mechanics of weather itself.
In short, correcting this seemingly minor conceptual misstep—endeavoring to attribute causation to phenomena that are capable of being causative—transforms our understanding from confusion to clarity. Recognizing that differences are not inherently causal—but rather indicators of deeper causal mechanisms—is the essential first step in finally unraveling the long-misunderstood mysteries of water, weather, and storms.
