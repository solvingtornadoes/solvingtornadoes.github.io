Yes, and given the fact there is something like 50 to 90 times more liquid H2O (in a given parcel) in the troposphere and the spectrum for EME interaction (not just IR) is so much more comprehensive for liquid H2O than that of CO2 one has to ask how it was possible for anybody to indicate comparative equivalence between gaseous CO2 in the atmosphere and liquid H2O.  The thermal impact of gaseous CO2 in the atmosphere is miniscule compared to that of liquid H2O.  Yet many  people, on both sides of the AGW fence, believe that the net impact of CO2 is greater than the net impact of H2O.  Why is this?  What is the ultimate source of the confusion on this subject and why is this superstition (delusion) so persistent?  
I believe that a big part of the confusion on this subject is that people (many people) maintain the superstitious notion that H2O is gaseous in the atmosphere. And that since H2O is gaseous in the atmosphere that the mechanism of its heat capacity must be elasticity of its covalent bonds, which is complete nonsense (the actual mechanism of liquid H2O’s heat capacity is hydrogen bonds between molecules, not covalent bonds).  And since CO2 is also a tri-atomic molecule like H2O that such comparisons are valid.
