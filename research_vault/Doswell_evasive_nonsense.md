Traditional Mitigation of Severe Weather: Tornadoes, Hurricanes, and Cyclones
Mitigating the impact of severe weather such as tornadoes, hurricanes, and cyclones has long been a topic of scientific inquiry and debate. The goal is not only to predict these events with greater accuracy but also to develop methods to reduce their intensity and minimize the resulting damage. While complete control of such large-scale atmospheric phenomena remains beyond current technological capabilities, a variety of mitigation theories and strategies have been proposed, some of which are rooted in modifying atmospheric conditions, while others focus on enhancing community resilience.
Tornado Mitigation Theories and Strategies
Tornadoes are among the most challenging severe weather events to mitigate due to their rapid formation, small scale, and intense destructive power. Several theoretical approaches have been proposed to weaken tornadoes, though practical implementation remains highly experimental.
One theory centers around disrupting the conditions necessary for tornado formation, particularly focusing on modifying wind shear and instability. Howard Bluestein, a leading tornado expert, suggests that "disrupting the updraft or altering wind shear could theoretically prevent tornado genesis, but the difficulty lies in executing such modifications at the scale and speed required" (Bluestein, 2017). Researchers have explored the possibility of using localized heating or cooling to disrupt the thermal balance within a developing supercell. For instance, heating the surface layer to reduce the temperature differential that drives the updraft could potentially prevent the conditions that lead to tornado formation. However, this approach remains largely theoretical and poses significant logistical challenges, such as the scale of heating required and the unpredictability of tornado development.
Another proposed method is the use of explosive charges to disrupt the formation of the vortex. The idea is that a well-timed explosion could introduce turbulence into the incipient vortex, preventing it from organizing into a tornado. While this concept has been discussed since the mid-20th century, experts like Erik Rasmussen argue that "the energy involved in a tornado is far greater than that of any practical explosive device, making this approach highly unlikely to succeed in real-world conditions" (Rasmussen, 2018). Moreover, the ethical and safety concerns associated with detonating explosives near populated areas make this approach problematic.
A more promising avenue of research involves the use of drones and unmanned aerial systems (UAS) to interfere with tornado development. These drones could theoretically release aerosols or create localized areas of increased turbulence to disrupt the formation of a tornado. Christopher Weiss, a professor of atmospheric science, notes that "the use of drones to alter atmospheric conditions is a fascinating concept, but we are still far from understanding how to deploy such technology effectively during real-time tornado outbreaks" (Weiss, 2020).
Hurricane and Cyclone Mitigation Theories and Strategies
Hurricanes and cyclones present unique challenges due to their massive scale and long duration. The primary focus of hurricane mitigation has been on reducing the intensity of storms before they make landfall, thereby limiting the potential damage to coastal communities.
One prominent theory involves the use of cloud seeding to reduce hurricane intensity. Cloud seeding involves introducing substances such as silver iodide or salt into clouds to encourage precipitation, thereby reducing the amount of moisture available to fuel the storm. This idea gained traction in the 1960s with Project Stormfury, a series of experiments led by the United States government to weaken hurricanes by cloud seeding. However, the results were inconclusive, and many experts today believe that cloud seeding is unlikely to have a significant impact on well-developed hurricanes. As Hugh Willoughby, a former director of the Hurricane Research Division, states, "The energy in a mature hurricane is simply too vast to be significantly altered by cloud seeding techniques, which only affect a small part of the storm" (Willoughby, 2016).
Another approach is the use of ocean cooling to disrupt the energy supply to hurricanes. Hurricanes derive their energy from warm ocean waters, and reducing sea surface temperatures could theoretically weaken a storm. The concept of using large-scale ocean pumps to bring cold water from deeper layers to the surface has been proposed as a potential mitigation strategy. Kerry Emanuel, a prominent hurricane researcher, points out that "while ocean cooling is theoretically possible, the scale of such an operation would need to be enormous to have any measurable effect, and the unintended consequences on marine ecosystems could be significant" (Emanuel, 2018).
More recent proposals have focused on deploying large fleets of unmanned vehicles or platforms to spread biodegradable films over the ocean's surface. These films could reduce evaporation rates, thereby limiting the amount of moisture available to a hurricane. However, practical challenges, such as the scale of coverage required and the durability of the films under extreme conditions, remain significant obstacles.
The Role of Atmospheric Stabilization
A growing area of research in severe weather mitigation involves atmospheric stabilization, where the aim is to reduce the instability that leads to storm formation. This approach involves altering the atmospheric conditions that contribute to severe weather, such as temperature gradients and moisture availability.
One proposal involves the injection of aerosols into the stratosphere to reduce surface temperatures and thus decrease the intensity of hurricanes and cyclones. This concept is similar to geoengineering proposals aimed at combating climate change, and it has been suggested that reducing sea surface temperatures by just a few degrees could significantly weaken hurricanes. Alan Robock, a climate scientist, cautions that "geoengineering techniques, including aerosol injection, carry a high risk of unintended consequences, such as changes in regional weather patterns and impacts on agricultural productivity" (Robock, 2018).
Another theory is the potential use of wind turbines to reduce hurricane intensity. Studies have suggested that large arrays of offshore wind turbines could extract kinetic energy from hurricanes, thereby reducing their wind speeds before they reach land. Mark Jacobson, a professor of civil and environmental engineering, conducted simulations indicating that "a large number of wind turbines could reduce wind speeds by up to 50% in some cases, but the practicality and cost of deploying thousands of turbines in hurricane-prone areas remain major barriers" (Jacobson, 2014).
Challenges and Future Directions
Despite the numerous theories and proposals for mitigating severe weather, significant challenges remain. The primary issue is the sheer scale and energy involved in storms like tornadoes, hurricanes, and cyclones. The energy released by a hurricane, for example, is equivalent to several nuclear bombs detonating every minute, making any attempt to alter such a system incredibly daunting.
Furthermore, the unpredictability of severe weather adds another layer of complexity. Even if a theoretically viable method of mitigation were developed, deploying it effectively would require near-perfect timing and precision, which is difficult to achieve given the chaotic nature of weather systems. As Edward Lorenz famously demonstrated, small changes in initial conditions can lead to vastly different outcomes, meaning that any intervention could have unintended consequences that are difficult to predict.
Ultimately, the focus of severe weather mitigation may need to shift towards enhancing resilience rather than attempting to alter the storms themselves. This includes improving building codes, developing better early warning systems, and investing in community preparedness. While the idea of weakening hurricanes or preventing tornadoes is appealing, the current state of science suggests that our best bet for reducing the impact of severe weather lies in adaptation and preparedness.
In the words of Roger Pielke Jr., an expert in environmental policy, "We may never be able to stop hurricanes or tornadoes, but we can certainly reduce their impact by making smarter decisions about where and how we build, and by ensuring that people are better prepared to respond when severe weather strikes" (Pielke, 2019).
The path forward will likely involve a combination of advancing our understanding of storm physics, developing novel technologies for mitigation, and enhancing community resilience. As our climate changes and severe weather events potentially become more frequent and intense, the need for effective mitigation strategies becomes ever more urgent.

The Evolution of Storm Physics: Thunderstorms, Tornadoes, and Hurricanes
The study of storms has evolved through centuries of scientific curiosity, trial and error, and significant breakthroughs that have shaped our understanding of atmospheric phenomena. From the early musings of Benjamin Franklin to the more sophisticated chaos theories of Edward Lorenz, storm physics has traveled a winding path. This essay delves into the evolution of storm physics, highlighting the contributions of key figures, identifying the gaps in current knowledge, and comparing competing theories about atmospheric vortices.
The Early Pioneers of Storm Study
The history of storm science is deeply rooted in the observations of early pioneers. Benjamin Franklin, known as one of America's founding fathers, also made fundamental contributions to our understanding of thunderstorms. In the 18th century, Franklin conducted groundbreaking experiments with electricity. His iconic kite experiment in 1752 demonstrated that lightning was a form of electrical discharge. Franklin's work laid the foundation for the study of electrical properties of storms, sparking interest in understanding atmospheric conditions beyond just observing their effects.
Moving forward to the early 19th century, Francis Beaufort introduced a more structured way of describing atmospheric conditions. A naval officer and hydrographer, Beaufort developed the Beaufort Wind Force Scale in 1805, a descriptive system for gauging wind speeds based on the effects observed at sea and on land. Though the scale did not directly involve storm physics, it provided a systematic way to observe atmospheric disturbances, laying an essential groundwork for storm analysis.
The significant leap towards understanding the chaotic nature of atmospheric systems came with Edward Lorenz, a meteorologist and mathematician whose work in the mid-20th century revolutionized meteorology. Lorenz's insights into chaos theory, and his famous "butterfly effect," underscored the inherent unpredictability of weather systems. Lorenz demonstrated that small changes in initial atmospheric conditions could lead to vastly different outcomes, making long-term weather forecasting a daunting challenge. His 1963 paper, "Deterministic Nonperiodic Flow," introduced the concept of deterministic chaos, which illustrated how weather systems are highly sensitive to initial conditions. This concept fundamentally altered the way meteorologists approached weather prediction, emphasizing the limits of precise forecasting and highlighting the need for probabilistic approaches. As Lorenz himself put it, "Two states differing by imperceptible amounts may eventually evolve into two considerably different states" (Lorenz, 1963).
Identifying Gaps in Current Knowledge
Despite centuries of study, our understanding of storm systems like thunderstorms, tornadoes, and hurricanes still contains significant gaps. Traditional meteorological models often struggle to fully explain the dynamics of atmospheric vortices. For instance, while we understand the general conditions that lead to tornado formation—such as wind shear, instability, and moisture—the precise mechanisms that determine why some thunderstorms produce tornadoes while others do not remain elusive. As Paul Markowski, a leading tornado researcher, notes, "We still don't fully understand the exact sequence of events that transforms a thunderstorm into a tornadic supercell" (Markowski, 2020).
Another significant gap lies in understanding the rapid intensification of hurricanes. While scientists can identify factors like sea surface temperature, humidity, and atmospheric instability as key drivers, the precise sequence of events that allows some systems to intensify rapidly while others dissipate is not fully understood. Kerry Emanuel, a prominent hurricane expert, states, "Our ability to predict rapid intensification is still quite limited, and this remains one of the most challenging aspects of hurricane forecasting" (Emanuel, 2018). This knowledge gap limits our ability to predict rapid intensification, which is critical for preparing communities in hurricane-prone regions.
There are also uncertainties regarding the role of microphysical processes within storm systems. For example, the behavior of cloud droplets and ice crystals, and how they interact with the surrounding environment, remains an area of active research. Hugh Morrison, an atmospheric scientist, points out that "Microphysical processes, such as the formation of graupel and hail, are not well represented in current models, leading to significant uncertainties in storm intensity predictions" (Morrison, 2019). The complexity of these microphysical interactions, combined with the challenges of measuring them in situ, makes it difficult to accurately simulate storm dynamics.
The role of mesoscale convective systems (MCS) in storm development is another gray area. MCSs are clusters of thunderstorms that can span hundreds of kilometers and significantly influence weather patterns, including the development of tornadoes and hurricanes. However, the exact mechanisms by which MCSs interact with larger atmospheric conditions to trigger severe weather events are not fully understood. As Christopher Davis, a researcher at the National Center for Atmospheric Research, explains, "The interaction between mesoscale convective systems and their environment is complex and not yet fully captured by current numerical models" (Davis, 2021).
Comparing Different Theories
The study of atmospheric vortices has led to a variety of theories, each attempting to explain the intricate nature of storms. Traditional meteorological models view tornadoes, hurricanes, and thunderstorms primarily in terms of thermodynamic principles and fluid dynamics. These models emphasize the role of warm, moist air rising into cooler layers of the atmosphere, creating instability and, ultimately, the formation of vortices.
Alternative theories have also emerged, focusing on different aspects of storm dynamics. One notable theory is the "vortex breakdown" theory, which attempts to explain how tornadoes form and maintain their structure. This theory suggests that under certain conditions, a rotating updraft can experience a breakdown in its structure, leading to the formation of smaller, more intense vortices within the main circulation. Howard Bluestein, a leading expert in tornado dynamics, has contributed significantly to this field, suggesting that "vortex breakdown is a key process in the formation of multiple-vortex tornadoes" (Bluestein, 2017).
Another area of interest is the role of boundary layer processes in storm formation. The boundary layer, the lowest part of the atmosphere, plays a crucial role in the transfer of heat, moisture, and momentum between the surface and the atmosphere. However, the exact influence of boundary layer dynamics on storm initiation and intensity remains poorly understood. As Erik Rasmussen, a severe weather researcher, notes, "The interaction between the boundary layer and storm dynamics is one of the least understood aspects of severe weather, particularly in the context of tornado genesis" (Rasmussen, 2018).
The Path Forward
The historical evolution of storm physics reveals both progress and persistent challenges. From Franklin's early observations of electrical charges in thunderstorms to Lorenz's insights into chaos theory, our understanding of atmospheric phenomena has become increasingly sophisticated. Yet, significant gaps remain, particularly regarding the microphysical processes within storms, the rapid intensification of hurricanes, and the role of mesoscale convective systems.
Addressing these gaps requires an interdisciplinary approach that combines traditional meteorological techniques with advances in computational modeling, remote sensing, and in situ observations. Enhanced data collection, such as through the use of drones and high-resolution satellite imagery, could provide new insights into storm structure and evolution. Furthermore, continued development of probabilistic forecasting methods, inspired by Lorenz's work on chaos theory, will be essential for improving the accuracy of storm predictions. As our understanding deepens, the hope is that we can improve prediction models, enhance preparedness, and ultimately reduce the devastating effects of these powerful natural events.


Dry Layer Capping: My opinion
By James McGinn
The notion of dry layer capping is widely presented as fundamental to atmospheric science, especially in explaining the suppression of convection and the development of stable layers in the atmosphere. However, a closer examination raises legitimate questions about its scientific validity. Despite its theoretical importance, there is a striking lack of direct empirical detection or measurement of dry layer capping in the atmosphere. The explanations provided by conventional meteorology often appear dubious, as they seem designed to distract from the fact that no empirical evidence of dry layer capping has ever been directly observed in real atmospheric conditions.
Current methods rely heavily on indirect inferences through satellite observations, reanalysis data, and numerical weather prediction models. These approaches do not directly detect dry layer capping; rather, they rely on assumptions embedded within models that presume dry layers act as caps to convection. The lack of direct measurements raises serious concerns about whether dry layer capping, as conceptualized, actually plays the role it is purported to play in atmospheric dynamics.
Field experiments and observations, even those involving sophisticated instruments such as satellites, Doppler radars, and aircraft sensors, have yet to provide concrete empirical evidence of dry layer capping occurring as described in textbooks. Instead, what is often presented is an interpretation of atmospheric stability based on assumptions, rather than definitive measurements of the capping process. This is particularly concerning because it suggests that the concept may be more theoretical conjecture than scientific fact—a convenient placeholder rather than a phenomenon that has been objectively validated.
If dry layer capping truly underpins the suppression of convection and the stabilization of the atmosphere, why has there been no direct measurement or detection of this phenomenon in action? The continued reliance on indirect data and the absence of empirical verification hint at the possibility that the concept might be fundamentally flawed, or even entirely nonsensical. Perhaps it is time to scrutinize and reassess the validity of this notion, rather than accepting it as an unquestionable truth of atmospheric science.




When Science Misattributes Cause: How a Common Conceptual Error
Kept Water Science and Meteorology in the Dark for Decades
James McGinn / Solving Tornadoes
Can a misunderstanding about causation cause an entire scientific field to spin its wheels, going
nowhere for decades? Science, for all its rigor, is vulnerable to conceptual errors, and one of these
errors has profoundly hindered our understanding of water and, consequently, the very nature of
weather itself.
Consider a seemingly straightforward question: Can a difference, in and of itself, ever be causal?
Reflect carefully on this. It might seem intuitive—differences are constantly cited as reasons for
occurrences. But the correct answer is subtle yet crucial: a difference alone, whether qualitative
or quantitative, cannot be inherently causal. Common parlance frequently treats differences as
causal, but this shorthand typically presumes an underlying causal mechanism already
understood. Unfortunately, this linguistic shortcut has fostered a pervasive scientific
delusion—that differences alone can generate causation.
This conceptual error traces back to Linus Pauling's explanation for the polarity of water
molecules in the 1950s. Pauling famously stated that water's polarity is caused by the
electronegativity difference between its oxygen and hydrogen atoms. While it's true that oxygen
and hydrogen differ in electronegativity—and indeed, this difference is instrumental—this
difference itself does not directly cause the shift of electrons relative to the nuclei that defines
polarity. Instead, it's the electrical gradients emanating from the oxygen atom that shifts the
electron location on the hydrogen atoms, and conversely, it's the electrical gradients emanating
from Hydrogen atoms that shifts the electron location on the oxygen atom. Differences in
electronegativity cause the shift in the oxygen to be more negative and the shift in the hydrogen
to be more positive. Thus, the difference in electronegativity is instrumental but not
fundamentally causal. The true causal agents are these electrical gradients.
Why does this subtle distinction matter so deeply? Because when water molecules form hydrogen
bonds, their electrical gradients directly oppose each other. In keeping with the standard rules of
chemistry, when electrical gradients oppose one another they cancel out each other’s effect.
Specifically, with each and every hydrogen bond electrical gradients from both molecules are
brought into opposition, thereby neutralizing an incremental percentage of each other’s polarity,
25% per bond with each water molecule capable of forming up to four hydrogen bonds. Through
the polarity neutralizing effects of these “hydrogen bonds,” water acts as a solvent to its own
polarity, effectively turning polarity off and on incrementally with the formation and dissipation
(breakage) of hydrogen bonds. The failure to recognize this has led scientists to label the ensuing
unexpected behaviors that result from this mixture of hydrogen bonding causing polarity
neutralization and bond dissipation causing activation as "anomalous," perpetuating confusion
and misunderstanding.
Water, therefore, is not anomalous—it's simply misunderstood. The origin of this
misunderstanding lies in incorrectly attributing causation to a mere difference. Far from being
trivial, this error has had enormous implications, particularly for meteorology. Understanding
weather patterns and atmospheric vortices—what might be called the "plumbing" of our
atmosphere—depends fundamentally on understanding water’s surface tension.
At the top of the troposphere, moist and dry air meet under conditions known as wind shear.
Here, vortices form, as they grow, often trending downward along more vertical wind shear
boundaries, funneling energetic low pressure downward to produce uplift, cloud formation and
storms and siphoning air from lower altitudes upward, exhausting their flow into jet streams
(thereby explaining how vortices function to maintain the momentum of the flow of jet streams).
To grasp how these vortices arise and function, one must first appreciate surface tension. And,
along these lines, consider that this phenomenon, “surface tension” was originally labelled as
such because water molecules along the surface of liquid water are commonly observed to be
attached to each other, producing a thin, weak but structurally stable surface. We can now say
why: the surface of liquid water experiences incomplete hydrogen bonding—thus reactivating
polarity and generating the cohesive forces observed in the H2O molecules on the surface of
liquid water.
Hopefully you understand that what has been stated up to this point in this article is that to fully
comprehend surface tension one must first correctly understand water’s polarity. If the cause of
polarity is misattributed the entire conceptual framework collapses. Without recognizing that it is
electrical gradients—rather than merely differences in electronegativity—that create polarity, and
without understanding how hydrogen bonds incrementally neutralize this polarity, one cannot
grasp the full extent of water’s surface tension capabilities—capabilities which, in the context of
the unique conditions of atmospheric wind shear, are capable of much higher magnitude than
that associated with the relatively subtle form of surface tension that occurs on the surface of
liquid water. It is this higher magnitude form of surface tension (the details of which are outside
the scope of this article) which serves as the basis of the structural properties of atmospheric
vortices, and without which one cannot hope to decipher the fundamental mechanics of weather
itself.
In short, correcting this seemingly minor conceptual misstep—endeavoring to attribute causation
to phenomena that are capable of being causative—transforms our understanding from confusion
to clarity. Recognizing that differences are not inherently causal—but rather indicators of deeper
causal mechanisms—is the essential first step in finally unraveling the long-misunderstood

This notion has evolved into a taboo within the disciplines that study the atmosphere, the primary champions and enforcers of this taboo being meteorologists, most of whom for which the issue is a mute point in that they exclusively work with synoptic charts (cold fronts, warm fronts and such, usually displayed on computer screens) and, therefore, the notion is never applied in the context of their daily duties. Steam-based Convection Myth Fails to Predict


The story underlying the steam-based convection myth becomes stranger still when one considers how incredibly resilient this notion has been despite its failure to predict or anticipate new discoveries. Basically there have been three waves of new evidence (most of which being due to advances in aviation) that the steam-based convection model failed to predict.

The earliest of these three discoveries was the discovery of extreme turbulence at the top of thunderstorms. This was surprising because up until then it had been assumed that as water molecules within a parcel of air combined with other water molecules in the colder air at the top of the troposphere the air parcel would become more dense. And, consequently, it’s buoyancy–the assumed source of its power–would be neutralized. Additionally, when observed from the ground the tops of thunderstorms appeared to be benign, fluffy, and harmless. Forced to Choose Between Wrong and Ridiculously Wrong


As with the other shortcomings of the steam-based convection model that I delineated at the beginning of this post, this failure to predict the discovery of these three meteorological phenomena is not something for which meteorologists are remotely concerned or even generally aware. All in all, they demonstrate an amazing ability to pretend not to notice the shortcomings of their theory and even to conceal it by, as I indicated, hiding the notion of steam within the less plainly absurd notion of convection. And then, of course, there is the evasiveness and name calling directed at anybody that doesn’t play along with their desire to pretend not to notice. Rather, I suggest, they are mistaken on one point of fact that has resulted in them making an omission. And in that omission they have closed themselves off to to an element in their explanations without which it is impossible to make sense of what is actually observed in the atmosphere, leaving them to choose between explanatory approaches that are wrong and ridiculously wrong. And it all has to do with how we envision water getting up high into the sky.


Here’s the thing. The pervasiveness of H2O in all parts of the troposphere (from the surface all the way up to the stratosphere) is undeniable. Likewise, the pervasiveness of H2O in all weather events is equally undeniable. Moreover, H2O is constantly falling out of the sky. Thus, the number one job of a theoretical meteorologists is to explain how water, H2O, gets up into the sky. The explanatory elements that meteorologists must use to achieve this explanation are the following: dry air (N2 and O2, and other trace inert gasses) and the three phases of water, ice, liquid water, and gaseous water (steam). Obviously ice is not viable. Ice only falls out of the sky. Liquid water water is more viable in that it is commonly observed in the form of fog or clouds, visible droplets. And, starting from evaporation, these can stay suspended in the air due to electrostatic forces, which are implications of N2 and O2. However, electrostatic forces in and of themselves can only explain, at best, how liquid droplets of water (most are so small they are invisible). what we commonly refer to as humidity, can get up to about 1,000 meters, 1,500 meters at most. It is plainly observable what does the remainder of the work to get moisture up above 7,000 meters–storms. And storms do this in a relatively rapid and dramatic manner, producing the sometimes violent updrafts that are such a hazard to aircraft. So the issue can be reduced to the question as to what powers storms? It would seem the answer must be obvious, buoyancy induced by steam, the gaseous phase of water, powers storms because there is nothing else that can possibly explain why moist air would begin to rise up through the drier surrounding air. Moreover, there is nothing else that can explain why storms themselves tend to be so wet, selectively occurring in moist air. Therefore, only steam can possibly explain how storms cause water–moist air–to rapidly get up above 7,000 meters. Right?


Actually, no. That is wrong. There is something else that can/will explain the uplift of moist air observed in storms. And it doesn’t involve steam whatsoever. In fact, the assertion that steam cannot exist in our atmosphere is, in fact, true. So the notion that steam provides the buoyancy of storms and/or the notion that buoyancy is the only force that can describe the uplift in storms and/or the notion that the only way water can provide uplift is through buoyancy are all false statements. There is another force involved. There is another way in which water can provide uplift in storms. And the three atmospheric phenomena that I mentions above, 1) the turbulence at the top of thunderstorms, 2) the jet stream, and 3) clear-air turbulence, are big clues as to this other force. In a follow-up post to this post I will demonstrate how one can start from the observation of CAT and track back through the jet stream, through to the tops of thunderclouds all the way down to the moist air (humidity) at lower altitudes and perfectly explain how this moist air begins to rapidly rise up into the upper troposphere (over 7.000 meters). And at no time will this explanation require the existence of steam or buoyancy.

How do hydrogen bonds between water molecules compare and contrast with covalent and ionic bonds?


Hydrogen bonds between water molecules are categorically distinct from either covalent bonds or ionic bonds. As a means of comparison, ionic bonds involve one negative atom and one different positive atom. In hydrogen bonding between water molecules both of the molecules that participate in any bond are the same molecule. They are both H2O molecules.


The H2O molecule is said to be polar, but this is an oversimplification that will cause confusion if you don't understand the underlying cause of this "polarity" and the situational factors that cause variability in the magnitude of the force that is associated with this "polarity". Specifically, it is best to comprehend exactly why/how the H2O molecule is polar. Failure to fully comprehend why/how the H2O molecule is polar is the basis for much of the cognitive dissonance that dominates this subject.


The H2O molecule is polar as a result of its net electrical gradients and their effect on the location of its electron clouds on all three of its nuclei (one oxygen and two hydrogen) and that effect is to push these electron clouds off center so that they are stretched or flagged relative to their respective nuclei. It is this stretching of electron clouds relative to their respective nuclei that causes one end of the H2O molecule to be negative (the "oxygen" end) and the other end to be positive (the "hydrogen" end), this being the charge separation that brings us to describe the H2O molecules as "polar."


But this polarity is not static; it is actually a very tenuous thing. With respect to which, it is of utmost importance to realize that this polarity is not the result of the asymmetry or lopsideness of the arrangement of its atoms (if that was the case then its polarity really would be static--this being the biggest mistake conventional theorists are making). Rather this polarity is the result of the asymmetry or lopsideness of its electrical gradients, what I refer to hereafter as the H2O molecule's intrinsic electrical gradients. And this is especially important with repect to the fact that when an H2O molecule makes hydrogen bonds with adjacent H2O molecules additional electrical gradients are brought into play from this adjacent H2O molecule. And these additional electrical gradients, what I will refer to hereafter as incidental (or you could use "external" rather than "incidental") electrical gradients, oppose or neutralize the H2O molecules intrinsic electrical gradients. The net effect is that hydrogen bonds with adjacent H2O molecules recentralize the electron clouds of its atoms relative to their respective nuclei (and this happens in both of the H2O molecules that participate in the bond, but only 25% per bond per molecule). This relative recentering of an H2O molecule's atoms' electron clouds to their respective nuclei neutralizes a proportional amount of the "polar" force. (Remember the "polar force" is a result of the H2O molecule's atoms' electron cloud being off center from its atoms' nuclei.) And it can potentially neutralize 100% of this "polar" force in that each H2O molecule can make up to 4 hydrogen bonds, one each with four adjacent H2O molecules, reducing the polar force to zero--potentially. So, hydrogen bonds serve two functions. Firstly, they provide connectedness between H2O molecules and, secondly, they neutralize a fraction, up to 25% per bond, of the magnitude of the force thereof. Stated more briefly, hydrogen bonding in H2O engender an inverse relationship between the connectedness of H2O molecules and the force of that connectedness.


It is the inverse relationship that underlies the peculiar properties of H2O--this being what has everybody perplexed. And, I suppose, this is why the whole discipline is ensconced in cognitive dissonance, nobody being a bigger victim and generator of mis-thinking than Gerald Pollack.
