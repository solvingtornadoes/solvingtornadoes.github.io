Abstract
Traditional explanations for water's unusual behavior have focused on the difference in electronegativity between oxygen and hydrogen. In contrast, our proposed framework asserts that molecular polarity is chiefly determined by the interactions among the intrinsic electrical fields of individual atoms. While electronegativity does play a role, it is really the mutual and vectorial interactions of these fields—reshaping the electron density—that give rise to a molecule's polarity. This refined understanding enables researchers to view hydrogen bonding as a dynamic “proximity switch.” When water molecules form hydrogen bonds, their opposing electrical fields partially cancel each other out, which reduces the overall polarity; when these bonds break, full polarity is restored. This mechanism offers a cohesive explanation for more than 70 persistent anomalies in water’s behavior.

Discussion
Rethinking Molecular Polarity
The classical view—championed by Linus Pauling in the 1950s—attributes the polarity of water to the inherent electronegativity differences between oxygen and hydrogen. However, this perspective fails to account for numerous anomalies observed in water’s physical properties. We contend that the primary determinant of a molecule’s polarity is not simply electronegativity differences but rather the interplay of the electrical fields produced by these individual atoms.
As a consequence of being brought into close proximity to each other, when two hydrogen atoms bond covalently to oxygen, their electric fields induce a redistribution of electron density on the oxygen, and reciprocally, oxygen’s electric field does the same on the hydrogen atoms. This asymmetric interaction results in a polar molecule; however, the conventional attribution to electronegativity differences alone overlooks the dynamic contribution of these electric field interactions.
Hydrogen Bonding as a “Proximity Switch”
Our model introduces hydrogen bonding as a mechanism that modulates polarity. When two water molecules form a hydrogen bond, segments of their respective electrical fields oppose each other, resulting in partial cancellation of the net polar force. This "proximity switch" mechanism is reversible—once the hydrogen bond dissociates, full polarity is reinstated. This dynamic modulation of polarity explains water’s perplexing characteristics, characteristics that heretofore have been dismissed as, “anomalous.”
Visualizing the Mechanism: A Heuristic Approach
In Figures 1 through 7, we present a simplified model that employs a heuristic atom to clarify hydrogen bonding in polar molecules. There is more than one reason for taking this heuristic approach. Traditional explanations of hydrogen bonding in H₂O have often left their audience confused. Undoubtedly this confusion is largely due to the overlooked “proximity switch” aspect of hydrogen bonding, the explanation for which is being addressed in this examination. But this is not the only factor that underlies this confusion, in our opinion. Another factor, we assert, is the complexity of H₂O’s tetrahedral oxygen arrangement, which provides four hydrogen bonding sites. In order to temporarily sidestep this complexity we will use a heuristic device, this being our “Heuristic Atom,” which we will employ as a substitute for oxygen in our explanation of this “proximity switch” aspect of hydrogen bonding (See figure One).

While the chart clearly shows that hydrogen bonds are much weaker than ionic bonds, one notable property of hydrogen bonds is their resilience or elasticity when broken; as the bonded atoms bounce against each other and move apart, the attractive forces actually increase. This behavior is evident from the slopes that terminate at 25% and especially at 0% in Figure Seven, which contrasts with the behavior seen in the hydrogen bond terminating at 50% and in the ionic bond. Consequently, substances held together by ionic bonds, although inherently stronger, are more prone to breaking, tearing, or shattering under stress. In contrast, the more elastic hydrogen bonds, especially those that terminate at 0%, are better at distributing stress to neighboring molecules, yielding a collective resilience.
Another characteristic demonstrated by the Heuristic molecule is the inverse relationship between cluster size and bond strength within the cluster. For example, if six Heuristic molecules are arranged linearly (with five bonds connecting them) the central molecules would experience zero net force, while the end connections would only contribute 25% of the force. This results in a loosely connected structure. However, if the same six molecules are divided into three pairs, each bond in these pairs would exhibit 50% strength, leading to a more robust connection despite fewer bonds overall. This phenomenon supports the assertion that there is an inverse relationship between the comprehensiveness of hydrogen bonding and the strength of hydrogen bonds—a factor that is even more pronounced in H₂O and underlies many of its "anomalies."

Water
Figures Eight through Eleven incorporate the full three-dimensional tetrahedral geometry of water (H₂O, see Figure One). In water, each hydrogen bond reduces overall polarity by 25%, and each H₂O molecule can form up to four hydrogen bonds with its neighbors. For instance, in liquid water, extensive bonding nearly neutralizes the polarity completely (see Figure Eight).
In contrast to the Heuristic molecule—whose potential for resilient bonding is limited by its linear, short polymer chains and the 180° electron configuration—the water molecule offers much greater possibilities. Its tetrahedral arrangement, with bond angles of 109.5°. This means that water molecules can display a far greater range of hydrogen bonding arrangements and three-dimensional interactions than our heuristic molecule.

Figure Eight: Liquid Water
Liquid water is characterized by a high degree of connectivity and, therefore, a high degree of tetrahedral symmetry and ensuing very low polarity in the bulk, except at the surface where at least 25% polarity remains due to the reduced three-dimensional connectivity—this gives rise to surface tension. 
________________
These factors underlie the high heat capacity of liquid water.  Hydrogen bonds conserve energy by “zeroing out” polarity as they hit against each other, bouncing, reactivating polarity as they move away, to get pulled back again, over and over, producing a cyclical elastic effect (between r1 and r2, see Figure Seven) that is the mechanism of water's high heat capacity. All in all, tetrahedral symmetry results in very low viscosity across the liquid temperature range, 0C to 100C, even extending below 0c to produce supercooled water, if the right situational factors are in effect (more on this below).

Figure Nine: Ice
In ice polarity is both higher and more variable than in liquid water. Although connectivity is moderate the strength of this connectivity is higher, providing structural rigidity. The lower density of ice, which explains why it floats on water, is due to internal gaps or surfaces between clusters that are highly random.
________________


The common belief that ice involves a tetrahedrally ordered arrangement of water molecules or what has been referred to as a “lattice” is a myth.  If water molecules did have the ability or the tendency to form into a lattice or any kind of ordered arrangement they would eliminate each other's polarity. And thereby the structural properties of ice would be impossible.
The transition from liquid water to solid ice is very abrupt. The activation of polarity as hydrogen bonds break allows remaining bonds to be structurally strong. This is because of the cascade effect of hydrogen bonds acting as a switch as the lack of energy causes molecules to fall in, twisting against each other, breaking bonds, activating polarity, all the while becoming more disordered. 
When describing this transition from liquid to solid ice, we could also say that initial disorder tends to self-perpetuate under low energy conditions. Specifically, under low energy conditions, initial “occlusions”---local areas of relative asymmetry that occur in a frequency above some (unknown) threshold—causes molecules to fall in, twisting against each other, breaking bonds, activating polarity, all the while becoming more disordered and essentially causing more occlusions, producing the ensuing structural rigidity that we call ice.
To understand what is stated in the previous paragraph it may help to realize that liquid water will naturally tend to achieve higher degrees of tetrahedral symmetry over time.  In other words, if it is free of impurities and if it is not jostled over longer periods of time “occlusions” will tend to get processed out of liquid water.  And if the frequency of occlusions (local asymmetries) remain below some (unknown) threshold the liquid phase is essentially predetermined.  This means that the temperature can drop below the normal freezing temperature and it will not freeze, it will remain liquid. This is referred to as supercooled liquid water. This can be undone by jostling it or introducing impurities that introduce local asymmetries,  initiating a cascade effect of hydrogen bonds acting as a switch, allowing it to rapidly turn to ice.

Figure Ten: Steam
In steam, water reaches its highest polarity because the molecules are free from the moderating effects of hydrogen bonding. Water’s anomalously high boiling point is largely due to the significant energy needed to collectively overcome the collective elastic strength of its hydrogen bonds—a strength that is further enhanced under higher atmospheric pressures, leading to marked variations in boiling temperature with changes in atmospheric pressure.

Figure Eleven: Spinning Polymers of Vortice Plasma
Under wind shear conditions, atmospheric nanodroplets encounter air molecules and experience side glancing collisions that gradually accelerate their spin. Occurring in vast numbers, these rapidly rotating, rigid, highly polar, polymer-like structures collectively form a plasma that constitutes the outer sheath of atmospheric vortices. Once formed, these vortices conserve the wind shear along their inner surface, causing the continuity of the conditions that maintain the plasma, resulting in vortices being able to persist. Through a vacuum or siphon effect, these vortices channel energetic, low-pressure, from jet streams directly into storm systems.  The considerable difference of pressure from where air enters the tubular vortice, at locations of lower altitude and higher pressure, to where air exits the vortice, at locations of higher altitude and lower pressure, accelerates the velocity of the air moving through the vortice, sometimes reaching speeds upwards of 300 mph. At one and the same time (or especially after they gain momentum) these vortices continuously exhaust their flow into the same jet stream, helping to sustain the momentum of the jet stream’s river-like flow along the top of the troposphere.

Conclusion
Characterizing it as being comparable to a weak ionic bond, traditional models have dismissively focused on electronegativity differences to explain water’s molecular polarity. This new perspective allows for a more nuanced understanding of water’s complex behaviors. Rather than envisioning hydrogen bonds between water molecules as static or solely driven by electronegativity differences, our model highlights the dynamic interplay of atomic electrical fields, recognizing hydrogen bonds as “proximity switches” that modulate molecular polarity. Accordingly, we provide a coherent framework for understanding over 70 anomalies of water—from the low viscosity, high heat capacity, and extreme sensitivity to atmospheric pressure of its boiling temperature to the structural rigidity associated with surface tension, ice and rigid polymers associated with atmospheric vortices, and even the elasticity and resilience of biological phenomena—thus offering a significant step forward in resolving long standing conceptual challenges in the study of polar molecular interactions.
Final Thoughts
Many will resist the idea that hydrogen bonds function as proximity switches. For them, the subject seems simple: they begin by accepting Pauling’s explanation that polarity is caused by differences in electronegativity—though they often recall this only vaguely and require a refresher. From there, they quickly settle on the notion that hydrogen bonds are just weak ionic bonds, brushing aside any contradictory evidence as mere anomalies. When this framework is challenged, they often respond with hostility, accusing the challenger of misunderstanding theoretical parsimony, and invoking Einstein or Feynman to defend the supposed elegance of their theory.
What they rarely confront is the deeper issue: any theory that accumulates over seventy unresolved anomalies is, by definition, broken.
My hope is that after reading and reflecting on this paper, you’ll see what became clear to me more than twenty years ago—classifying hydrogen bonds as weak ionic bonds is not only incorrect, it actively obstructs scientific progress. Consider storm physics: without recognizing that hydrogen bonds act as polarity-neutralizing switches, one cannot begin to grasp how vortice plasma transfers low-pressure energy from high-altitude jet streams to storms. This realization holds the key to both understanding the origin and potentially how to mitigate severe weather phenomena.
