Accounting for Lorenz’s Missing Lubrication in the Atmosphere
 James McGinn

For a long time, we've understood the source of energy that powers the winds: differential air pressure caused by the uneven heating and cooling of the Earth’s surface by the sun, further intensified by friction from geographical features like mountains and valleys. Yet, the mystery lies in how and why this energy creates concentrated flows, resulting in the high wind speeds observed in jet streams and storms. In nature, processes typically don't reverse entropy—going from a disorganized to an organized state—without a physical reason. A river is funneled into a stream by the contours of the landscape. So, what creates the atmospheric "hills and valleys"? What is the elusive substance responsible for these formations, and why is it so hard to detect?
These are essentially three questions, and here are my answers:
1. The atmospheric "hills and valleys" occur along wind shear boundaries, especially in the upper troposphere and tropopause, where the boundary conditions between moist and dry wind shear are prevalent, and in inversion layers in the lower atmosphere.
2. The substance is a water-based plasma that forms along these wind shear boundaries. This plasma arises from H₂O's surface tension and the behavior of spinning microdroplets. These droplets, sustained by wind shear, maximize their surface area, and as their surface area increases, so does their surface tension.
3. The reason this phenomenon is so fleeting is that it only manifests under high-energy conditions, which are relatively rare at the surface (as seen in tornadoes and hurricanes) and largely obscured by clouds at the tropopause.
Thus, jet streams and their smaller branches are vortices well-suited for transporting moist air over great distances, as in jet streams, or upwards, as seen in storms. These jet streams act as both mechanisms of uplift and distribution for moist air. Starting as sheets along wind shear boundaries between moist and dry air masses, they twist and wrap around the main flow due to the Bernoulli effect and the Coriolis effect. Jet streams are essentially tubes of plasma formed from spinning, surface-maximized microdroplets. Heavier and more viscous than the surrounding air, this plasma forms a sheath that channels the flow of high-speed air, functioning like a conduit that isolates it from atmospheric friction. This friction isolation is what allows jet streams to achieve such high wind speeds. Additionally, since H₂O surface tension is hydrophobic, it further reduces friction for the heavier, non-spinning microdroplets carried within the main flow.
In 1967, Edward D. Lorenz conducted calculations that suggested there should be significantly more friction in the atmosphere than what was observed. He proposed that some form of atmospheric "lubrication" had yet to be accounted for. I believe I’ve found it. It's the jet streams and their tributaries—or more specifically, the frictionless environment created by the water-based plasma that forms the vortex sheath, conserving the energy within the flow.
This explanation also clarifies why jet streams are positioned at the boundary between the moist troposphere and the dry stratosphere. For the spinning microdroplets that create the plasma to form and persist, the moisture must predominantly exist on one side (the troposphere) and not both, as opposing spins would cancel each other out. Therefore, the boundary needs to be moist on one side and dry on the other to sustain the spinning droplets that maximize H₂O surface tension, generating the plasma that forms the vortices we know as jet streams. These vortices are also related to other atmospheric phenomena like tornadoes and hurricanes, which can be seen as the volatile, whipping tails of the larger and more controlled force—the jet streams.

3. Why It’s Hard to Detect: This phenomenon is transient, only manifesting under high-energy conditions. At the Earth's surface, these conditions are rare and typically seen only in extreme weather events such as tornadoes and hurricanes. In the upper atmosphere, particularly near the tropopause, the phenomenon is largely obscured by cloud cover.
Jet streams and their smaller branches are, in essence, vortices that facilitate the transport of moist air over large distances or vertically, as seen in storms. These structures begin as sheets of air along the boundaries between moist and dry air masses, but through the combined influence of the Bernoulli and Coriolis effects, they twist and wrap into tubular formations. These jet streams are tubes of plasma composed of spinning microdroplets that maximize surface tension. Heavier and more viscous than the surrounding air, this plasma sheath serves as a conduit, isolating high-speed air from the atmospheric friction that would otherwise dissipate its energy. The hydrophobic nature of H₂O surface tension further reduces friction for the non-spinning microdroplets within the main flow, enhancing the plasma’s frictionless characteristics.
In 1967, Edward Lorenz postulated that atmospheric friction should be far greater than what observations suggested. He speculated that some form of atmospheric "lubrication" must exist but had not yet been identified. I believe that jet streams and their tributaries—specifically, the frictionless environment created by water-based plasma vortices—are that missing lubrication. The plasma sheath conserves energy within these flows by shielding them from friction, enabling the jet streams to reach their remarkable wind speeds.
This model also explains the positioning of jet streams at the boundary between the moist troposphere and the dry stratosphere. The persistence of the spinning microdroplets responsible for plasma formation requires that moisture exist on only one side of the boundary. Moisture on both sides would lead to opposing spins, which would cancel each other out. Thus, the distinct boundary—moist on one side, dry on the other—creates the necessary conditions for the plasma, which in turn forms the vortices we recognize as jet streams. These vortices are intimately related to other atmospheric phenomena, such as tornadoes and hurricanes, which can be viewed as more volatile offshoots of the jet streams' larger, more stable forces.
