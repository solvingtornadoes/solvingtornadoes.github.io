This discussion aims to elucidate the relationship between differences in atomic electronegativity and the resulting electrical gradients that give rise to molecular polarity. We begin by considering a model diatomic molecule in which two atoms with distinct electronegativities form a covalent bond. This bond establishes an uneven distribution of electron density that creates measurable electrical gradients and net partial charges, thereby defining the molecule as polar.
A central issue under consideration is whether the polarity of such a molecule should be primarily attributed to the difference in electronegativity between its constituent atoms, or rather to the resultant electrical gradients established by this difference. It is postulated here that one of these interpretations more accurately captures the underlying mechanism of polarity—a point that was overlooked in Pauling’s original framework.
Furthermore, the analysis extends to intermolecular interactions, specifically hydrogen bonding between polar molecules. Hydrogen bonds are demonstrated to function as a regulatory mechanism or "switch" that can modulate the effective polarity of a molecule. In addition, the spatial arrangement of non-bonded adjacent atoms can lead to the neutralization of local electrical gradients. This phenomenon is not restricted to isolated molecules; rather, it is also evident among molecules that are covalently bonded, thereby implying that hydrogen bonding can effectively attenuate the net polar force by approximately 50%. Conversely, disruption of these hydrogen bonds reactivates the full expression of the molecular polarity.
Recognizing polarity solely as a consequence of intrinsic electronegativity differences may obscure the dynamic nature of polarity modulation via hydrogen bonding. A more comprehensive understanding recognizes that the polarity of a molecule is inherently variable and subject to reversible changes governed by the formation and disruption of hydrogen bonds.
The operational principles of these interactions are inherently based on molecular proximity. This is illustrated by comparing the magnitude of electrical interactions in hydrogen-bonded systems with those characteristic of ionic bonds, as depicted in a corresponding XY graph. Such a comparison underscores that these interactions do not contradict Coulomb’s law but rather complement it by revealing an additional layer of complexity in molecular interactions.
Moreover, the elasticity observed in the behavior of polar molecules, such as water, can be interpreted through this framework. For example, the anomalously low viscosity of H₂O and its high heat capacity can be linked to the energy dynamics associated with the modulation of polarity via hydrogen bonding. Finally, the implications of these mechanisms extend to other macroscopic phenomena, such as the high surface tension of water.
In summary, the analysis presented here challenges Pauling’s original assumption regarding the origin of molecular polarity by emphasizing the role of hydrogen bonds as dynamic switches. This perspective not only reconciles various anomalous physical properties of polar substances but also enriches our understanding of molecular interactions in complex systems.
