Because that's approximately the main message (or a part of it) that I wanted to give during the podcast. Take sand, for instance. One grain of sand is solid, but many grains of sand act more like a liquid. Depending on whether you consider the atoms or the grains as constituents, you get a different behavior, and, as it were, a different aggregate state. Many physicists will refuse to call the sand a liquid, but will have no problem using liquid models to describe its behavior. In the end, it's all a matter of definition, but choosing new definitions can open up new perspectives, and a better understanding of the underlying physics.  I therefore fully support your idea of expanding the definition of plasma to include "non-ionic plasmas". As long as it's clear which aspect of the system is referred to as plasma, such broadened definition seems useful to me.
