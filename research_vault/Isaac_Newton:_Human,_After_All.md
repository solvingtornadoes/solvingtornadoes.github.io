Let’s start with a big question: Was Isaac Newton some all-knowing, infallible genius? Or was he, like the rest of us, prone to the occasional blunder? Personally, I’m betting on the latter. But if you’ve read the USA Today article, "Understanding Air Density and Its Effects," you’d be forgiven for thinking Newton never made a misstep. Spoiler alert: they’re wrong.
The article tackles an interesting misconception — that moist air is heavier than dry air. According to the article, this is a myth. But here’s the twist: in reality, moist air is heavier than dry air. The true myth is the idea that it’s lighter, a misconception academia has been pushing for centuries. And guess who’s at the root of it? None other than Isaac Newton himself, with a statement in his 1717 book, Optics.
So how did Newton—and academia—get it wrong? According to USA Today, it all starts with Avogadro’s law. Let’s break it down:
USA Today: "To see why humid air is less dense than dry air, we turn to one of the laws of nature discovered by the Italian physicist Amadeo Avogadro. He found that a fixed volume of gas, at the same temperature and pressure, contains the same number of molecules, no matter what gas is in the container."
James McGinn: That’s all well and good, but there’s one small problem. At ambient temperatures and pressures, water (H₂O) isn’t a gas. It’s not flying solo as individual molecules—it’s more like a vapor, made up of tiny liquid microdroplets, held aloft by electrostatic forces between air molecules. These microdroplets are so small they’re invisible, which tricks people into thinking they’re looking at gaseous H₂O. But here’s the kicker: the idea that water exists as a true gas in our atmosphere is pure fiction. Lab evidence shows that gaseous H₂O can only exist at temperatures above boiling point—way higher than what we experience on a typical day.
Let’s keep going.
USA Today: "Dry air is mostly nitrogen (78%), with a molecular weight of 28, and oxygen (21%), with a molecular weight of 32. Toss in a few trace gases and voilà: that’s your atmosphere."
James McGinn: So far, so good. Now, here’s where things go off the rails:
USA Today: "When water vapor is added to air, some of the nitrogen and oxygen molecules leave. Since water vapor molecules have a molecular weight of 18, they’re lighter than nitrogen and oxygen. Replacing these gases with water vapor decreases the weight of the air—so, humid air is less dense than dry air."
James McGinn: Whoa, hold up. They say the molecular weight of water vapor is 18. But remember those microdroplets I mentioned earlier? The actual number we should be using isn’t 18—it’s 18 multiplied by X, with X representing the number of H₂O molecules in each microdroplet. And what’s X? Well, we don’t know for sure, but it’s likely never smaller than 10. So the real number we should plug into the equation is more like 180 or higher. Even in super-dry air, where X might be as small as 3, that would still give us a molecular weight of 54, making moist air much heavier than dry air.
So the next time someone tries to tell you that moist air is lighter than dry air, you can let them in on the secret: that so-called "myth" is actually the real myth! And as for Newton? Turns out, he wasn’t the all-knowing genius we’ve built him up to be—just another human who didn’t quite nail every assumption.
